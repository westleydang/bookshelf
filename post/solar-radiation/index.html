not always hottest at the equator

Jun 2, 2017 13:43 · 279 words · 2 minutes read

I was in Sweden and I’m pretty sure that the sun was more intense up there, on account that I tanned faster than when I was in San Diego. My friend begged to differ, arguing that the sun was always most intense at the equator.

Well, I don’t know anything about that, except for that my first-grade teacher told me the very same thing.

But what I do know are these:

  • The Earth is on a 23.5˚ axial tilt, and simple trig would suggest that the solar radiation is most direct at +/- 23.5˚ of the equator.
  • During the Northern summer, the most direct solar radiation hits at 23.5˚ North of the equator.
  • More direct angles to the surface of the Earth means less atmospheric scattering.
  • The atmosphere stretches to 500 km above the Earth’s surface but most of it is within 15 km.
  • UV at high altitude (even at 5,000 km) is much more intense despite it only being a few miles above sea level.
  • The shape of the Earth is oblate, so the equator stretches closer to the Sun, but this is negligible for distance, and if anything, it means that the most direct angle is actually less than 23.5˚ latitude.
  • Solar intensity follows the inverse square law (so any additional distance follows a square loss in intensity).

All together, this would lead me to believe that:

  1. The Northern summer has more intense sunlight at the Tropic of Cancer than at the equator.
  2. The Tropic of Capricorn has more intense sunlight during the Southern summer than the Tropic of Cancer during the Northern summer.

Turns out, I was partially right, but only because Sweden is at 55˚N.
